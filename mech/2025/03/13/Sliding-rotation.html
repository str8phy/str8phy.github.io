Sliding Rotation

Mar 13, 2025

So far rotations are the heart of mechanics for me.

The following simple example exploits following concepts:

  • moving pivot
  • restricted motion
  • accelerated & inertial frames of reference
  • noncontinuous forces
  • analogy between gravity force of vertical pendulum & friction force in the following setup

Scope

  1. Setup
  2. Identifying Forces
  3. Adding Friction

Setup

The setup is depicted on fig.1: \(x_1\) starts moving forward at \(t=t_0\) with speed being constant at \(t_0<t<\infty\).

Fig.1 setup: \(x_1\) and \(x_2\) linked by rod, \(x_1\) starts moving with constant speed

The problem is to describe the motion of \(x_2\).

Intuitively, \(x_2\) is expected to align its direction gradually with the that of \(x_1\). This can actually be checked by plugging two bounded buttons across a table desk. Produced motion will likely look more or less like this:

Fig.2 Typical motion with friction

Rotational kinematics

Exploiting the restriction of \(x_2\) being bound to \(x_1\) one might consider reference frame (r.f.) \(\mathcal{S}_1\) with origin at \(x_1\) alongside with inertial r.f. \(\mathcal{S}_0\) (with origin at some fixed point, say \((0,0)\)).

Motion of \(x_2\) with resp. to \(\mathcal{S}_0\) is obviously a sum of its motion with resp. to \(\mathcal{S}_1\) and a motion of \(x_1\) in \(\mathcal{S}_0\):

\[x_2 \left.\right|_{\mathcal{S}_0} = x_2 \left.\right|_{\mathcal{S}_1} + x_1 \left.\right|_{\mathcal{S}_0}, \qquad \;(1)\]

Following the restrictions imposed by setup, all possible trajectories of \(x_2\) in \(\mathcal{S}_1\) lay on circle around \(x_1\), thus may be described by the rotational operator \(R_\varphi\):

\[R_\varphi = \left(\begin{array}{lr} \cos\varphi & -\sin\varphi\\ \sin\varphi & \cos\varphi\end{array}\right),\]

i.e. \(x_2\lvert_{\mathcal{S}_1} = R_\varphi \left[\begin{array}{c} l\\0 \end{array}\right].\)

For the expression of derivatives of \(x_2\) it is to note that \(\cos\varphi\sim1, \sin\varphi\sim\varphi\) for small \(\varphi\). Thus

\[dR_\varphi x = \left(\begin{array}{lr} 0&-d\varphi\\d\varphi&0\end{array}\right)x = \dot{\vec{\varphi}}\times x,\]

where \(\vec{\varphi}\) is thought to be a vector having amplitude \(\varphi\) and being pointed along the pivot axis that passes through \(x_1\).

The derivatives of \(x_2\lvert_{\mathcal{S}_1}\) can then be written as follows:

\[\begin{array}{l} \dot{\vec{x}}_2 \left.\right|_{\mathcal{S}_0} = \dot{\varphi}\times R_\varphi \left[\begin{array}{c} l\\0 \end{array}\right] + \left[\begin{array}{c} v\\0 \end{array}\right],\\ \ddot{\vec{x}}_2 \left.\right|_{\mathcal{S}_0} = \ddot{\varphi}\times R_\varphi \left[\begin{array}{c} l\\0 \end{array}\right] + \dot{\varphi}\times\left( \dot{\varphi}\times R_\varphi \left[\begin{array}{c} l\\0 \end{array}\right]\right). \end{array}\]

Note that the first component of both \(\dot{\vec{x}}_2\lvert_{\mathcal{S}_1}\) and \(\ddot{\vec{x}}_2\lvert_{\mathcal{S}_1}\) is a vector tangential to the circle periphery, but \(\dot{\vec{x}}_1\lvert_{\mathcal{S}_1}\) also comprises a vector along horizontal axis while \(\ddot{\vec{x}}_1\lvert_{\mathcal{S}_1}\) comprises instead a vector pointed along the rod.

Identifying forces

Letâ€™s take a look on what provokes rotation of the rod at \(t=t_0\).

Multiplying eq.(1) by \(m\) the force seen at \(x_2\) is

\[m\ddot{\vec{x}}_2\left.\right|_{\mathcal{S}_1} = \vec{F}_2 \left.\right|_{\mathcal{S}_0} - m\ddot{\vec{x}}_1\left.\right|_{\mathcal{S}_0}, \qquad (2)\]

where \(\vec{F}_2\lvert_{\mathcal{S}_0}\) is essentially a reaction force along the rod, while \(\ddot{\vec{x}}_1\left.\right|_{\mathcal{S}_0}\) is pointed to right on our picture.

Accounting rotational nature of movement in \(\mathcal{S}_1\), we vector-multiply both sides of (2) by \(x_2\lvert_{\mathcal{S}_1}\) and then discard a component of \(x_2\lvert_{\mathcal{S}_1}\) being parallel to it:

\[l^2\ddot{\varphi} = \vec{x}_2\left.\right|_{\mathcal{S}_1} \times \vec{F}_1\left.\right|_{\mathcal{S}_0} \qquad (3)\]

By \(\vec{F}_1\) in (3) is designated a force that brings the whole thing to motion, namely increases the velocity of \(x_1\) from \(0\) to \(v\) inside some arbitrary time span \(\Delta t\). As \(\vec{F}\) is pointed to right on the setup plane, it comprises components pointed normally and tangentially to \(x_2\lvert_{\mathcal{S}_1}\). From notions on Fig.1 follows \(\vec{F}^\perp_1=F_1\sin\varphi_0=\frac{mv}{\Delta t}\sin\varphi_0\), with \(\varphi_0\) being the angle at \(t=t_0\).

Following (3) the change of \(\varphi\) that occurs during time span \(\Delta t\) is then

\[\Delta\dot{\varphi}=-\frac{v}{l}\sin\varphi_0.\]

Under frictionless assumption there are no torque-generating forces in \(S_1\), so rotation is not altered at \(t>t_0\) (i.e. the moment is conserved ).

Adding Friction

Things get more interesting when some friction is added in the mix. The following model is adopted for friction here:

\[\vec{f} = -k\dot{\vec{x}},\]

where \(\vec{f}\) is a friction force, \(k\) is a friction coefficient.

The motion in \(\mathcal{S}_1\) is then defined by

\[I_z\ddot{\varphi} = x_2\left.\right|_{\mathcal{S}_1} \times \vec{f} = \left[\begin{array}{r}-lcos\varphi\\l\sin\varphi\end{array}\right] \times \left[\begin{array}{l}-kv - k\dot{\varphi}l\sin\varphi\\-k\dot{\varphi}l\cos\varphi\end{array}\right] = -kl\left(v\sin\varphi + \dot{\varphi}l\right)\]

The latter equation describes the self-controlling property of the motion as the torque is always pointed counter-wise with the direction of rotation, i.e. the signs \(\ddot{\varphi}\) and \(\dot{\varphi}\) are opposite.
